Well, this is a silly thing to write about.

But it was just something I was thinking about earlier, and I wanted a place to put my thoughts down, and I wanted a place to do that, preferably where no one would ever see it. So, I made this site.

More than anything, I just want to express my admiration for the most venerable piece of hardware in my collection.

The Microsoft Comfort Curve 2000

![keyboard](/assets/comfortcurve2000.jpg)

This was an extremely common keyboard when it released back in the late 2000s. It was also quite cheap, I think about $25-30.

It's a full-size membrane keyboard with an ergonomic curve to the rows of keys and dedicated media buttons. 

I've tried many, many keyboard over the years in many shapes, styles, and hardware configurations. But I always come back to this one, and here's why:

The defining feature, the curve, not nearly as dramatic as Microsoft's other ergonomic keyboards. However, it does offer a noticeably different typing experience as opposed to a standard straight keyboard. I find it to be much more comfortable than a standard while also not completely dominating a desktop like the ergo boards do. It's the perfect blend between the two. 

The rest of the keyboard is standard full-size keyboard stuff, with the d-pad and the numpad, but they just feel like they're in the exact right spot. And I use the calculator button far more often than I ever would have expected. I use the home/end/pgup/pgdwn buttons so instinctively now that I don't think I would ever want to do real professional work without them. I even use the media keys from time to time. I will admit though, that the back/forward and web/search/mail buttons are basically useless.

It is very low-profile. The higher and taller a keyboard is, the more it hurts my wrists after long typing sessions. You're supposed to use a wristrest, but if you ever want to play some games, they become pretty annoying to constantly have to adjust. This keyboard is very low down, and the tilt is quite low as well. This really makes it more comfortable for me to type on than most mechanical keyboards.

The thing that brings it all together, the foundation upon which all else is built, the one thing a keyboard has to do right in order to be a good keyboard: the typing feel. Yes, it's a membrane keyboard, but it's the best feeling keypress of any board I've ever typed on. Stiff at the top, with a relatively high actuation force. But then as soon as you break through it drops effortlessly to the bottom. It almost feels as if the membrane starts actively pulling the key down in collaboration with you. The travel is shorter than a regular keyboard too. The result is a very punchy, quick keypress that leaves absolutely no doubt whether a key has been pressed while still being very fast to type on. 

I've tried so many other boards over the years, and none of them match what this one offers. I've tried low-profile mechanical keyboards, but the switches lack the same punchiness, and none of them come in an ergonomic curve.

Even without the form-factor consideration, the lack of a switch that feels as good to me is a huge negative to basically all mechanical switches I've tried. Blues were close but so noisy. Browns also were fairly good both they're still less stiff at the top, and required more force to get through to the bottom. 

I've tried other membrane keyboards, but they all feel like wading through mushy swamps in comparison.

Even Microsoft's own attempt at a follow-up, the Comfort Curve 3000 pales in comparison.

![keyboard](/assets/comfortcurve3000.jpg)

Overly shiny, overly swooped. It screams Windows Vista aesthetic. And look at that key staggering... the "W" is closer to "A" than it is to "S". Forget ever playing a game with WASD on this abomination.

IDK what it is, but the CC2000 just nailed it perfectly right for me coming up on 20 years ago. 

I will say that it isn't ideal for intense PVP games, as the keyboard is too wide and impedes the mousing area. I end up using a different keyboard for gaming.

So, over the years I've snapped up these keyboards whenever I see them. I think I have 3 spares out in a box in the garage in varying conditions. They do seem to develop this odd quirk when they get old that after being plugged in for a long time random buttons will stop working. To fix it I pick up one end of the keyboard about 4 inches above the desk and let it drop. I read somewhere the cause was static build-up or something. Well, this percussive maintenance seems to be etremely effective at clearing it up. My wife hates this. Thankfully the board I'm using now has not yet developed this idiosyncracy. 

I think there may be a bit of a cult following for them. Just now I looked on Amazon, and there's 2 listings and both of them are around $300. And used boards are $50+. 

God, what a dumb thing to write about.
